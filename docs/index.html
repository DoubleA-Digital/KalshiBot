How it works

An eight-layer pipeline, built in order

Each layer has one job and is validated before the next is built. The boring sequence is the point — you can't judge a strategy without a backtester, and you can't backtest without stored history.

🔌

Kalshi API client

RSA-PSS signed requests, rate-limited token bucket, full coverage of markets, orderbook, portfolio, and orders.

step 1 · shipped
💾

Data collection

Every tick persists market metadata, price snapshots, and optional orderbook depth into a versioned SQLite database.

step 2 · shipped
🧠

Pluggable strategies

A strategy returns (probability, confidence) for a market. Easy to swap, hard to overfit.

step 3 · next
📐

EV engine + Kelly

Compares strategy probability against current market price, nets out Kalshi fees, sizes with fractional Kelly.

step 4
🔁

Backtester

Replays any strategy over stored snapshots. Reports trades, win rate, P&L, predicted vs realized EV, max drawdown.

step 5
📝

Paper trading

Live data, simulated fills against a virtual bankroll. The default mode — real money never moves in development.

step 6
🛡️

Risk management

Hard limits regardless of strategy: max position, max exposure, daily loss auto-shutdown, kill switch.

step 7
⚙️

Autonomous loop

Ties everything together with a logged decision trail. Live trading lives behind one explicit, obvious flag.

Safety

Real money is off until you say otherwise

🔒

The live-trading guard

Calling place_order() on the Kalshi client raises immediately unless LIVE_TRADING=true is set in your environment. The guard lives in the API client itself — no later layer can route around it. The default mode is paper trading against a virtual bankroll, using real market data.

The bot does not claim to predict winners. It looks for trades where its modeled probability beats the market's price by enough to overcome fees and uncertainty — and it expects most markets, most of the time, to be priced correctly.
